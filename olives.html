A Practical Guide to Olives

The name "Ovilli" comes from the olive, so it is only fair that this site has a proper page about them. Olives are one of the oldest cultivated fruits in the world, and there is far more to them than the jar on a supermarket shelf. This guide covers why raw olives are inedible, how they are cured, the differences between the main varieties, how to read an olive oil label, and how to buy and store both olives and oil so they actually taste good.

Why you cannot eat an olive off the tree

Pick an olive straight from a branch and bite it, and you will immediately spit it out. Raw olives contain a compound called oleuropein, which is intensely bitter and astringent. Every edible olive you have ever eaten has been processed to remove or break down that bitterness. This single fact explains almost everything about how olives are produced: the entire craft of "curing" exists to turn an inedible fruit into something delicious.

How olives are cured

There are several traditional curing methods, and each one produces a different flavour and texture:

Brine curing

Olives are submerged in a salt-water solution for weeks or months. Natural fermentation slowly leaches out the bitterness and develops a complex, slightly sour flavour. This is how many Greek-style table olives are made.

Water curing

The olives are soaked in fresh water that is changed regularly. It is the gentlest method, keeps the fruit mild, and is often used before a final brine to round out the taste.

Lye curing

A food-grade alkaline solution rapidly neutralises the bitter compounds. It is the fastest method and is widely used for the smooth, mild black olives common on pizzas.

Dry (salt) curing

Olives are packed directly in salt, which draws out moisture and bitterness. The result is the wrinkled, intensely flavoured, slightly chewy olive often labelled "oil-cured".

Green or black: it is mostly ripeness

A common myth is that green and black olives are different fruits. In most cases they are the same olive picked at different stages: green olives are harvested early and are firmer and more bitter, while black olives are left to ripen on the tree and become softer and milder. Some commercially "black" olives are actually green olives that have been oxidised to a uniform dark colour — which is why they can taste flat compared with naturally ripened ones.

Olive varieties worth knowing

Kalamata

Almond-shaped, deep purple, and rich. A Greek classic, usually brine-cured, with a meaty bite that works in salads and slow-cooked dishes.

Manzanilla

The Spanish all-rounder. Firm, mild, and often stuffed. This is the olive most people picture when they think of a green table olive.

Castelvetrano

Bright green, buttery, and sweet, from Sicily. A great "gateway" olive for people who think they dislike olives.

Picholine

A small, crisp French variety with a fresh, slightly nutty taste. Popular as a snack and for pressing into oil.

Reading an olive oil label

Olive oil grades are not marketing words — they are defined quality tiers, and knowing them saves money:

  • Extra virgin — the top grade. Made by mechanical pressing only (no heat or chemicals), with very low acidity and no defects in taste. Best for finishing, dressings, and dipping.
  • Virgin — also unrefined and mechanically pressed, but with slightly higher acidity and minor flavour faults allowed.
  • Refined or "pure" olive oil — virgin oil that failed the taste test, then refined to remove defects. Cheaper, milder, fine for high-heat cooking.
  • Olive pomace oil — extracted from the leftover pulp using solvents and refining. The lowest grade; avoid for flavour.

A good tip: look for a harvest date, not just a best-before date. Olive oil is a fresh juice, not a wine — it does not improve with age. The fresher the harvest, the better it tastes.

How to store olives and oil

Keep olive oil in a cool, dark cupboard, tightly closed, and away from the stove. Its three enemies are heat, light, and air, all of which turn it rancid. Buy a size you will finish within a couple of months. Table olives keep for weeks in their own brine in the fridge; if you decant them, top them up with a little brine or olive oil so they stay submerged and do not dry out.

A quick history

Olive cultivation stretches back more than six thousand years around the Mediterranean. The trees are remarkably long-lived and drought-resistant — some are estimated to be over a thousand years old and still fruiting. Across many cultures the olive branch became a symbol of peace and continuity, and the tree itself a sign of endurance and a deep connection to the land. Not bad for a fruit you cannot even eat raw.
